## 1. GWAR Rules and Requirements

### Word Limits for Writing Tasks

**Individual Project Report:**
- **Minimum: 3,000 words** (main body of text only)
- **Automatic Zero Score:** If main body is less than 3,000 words
- **Page Requirement:** Minimum 12 pages for main body (excluding title page, references, etc.)

**Op-Ed Assignment:**
- Check specific assignment instructions for word count limits
- Typically ranges from 600-1,200 words for Op-Eds
- Must be concise and impactful

**Data Management Plan (DMP):**
- Check specific assignment instructions
- Usually 2-5 pages
- Must include all required sections

### Page Length Requirements

**Individual Project Report:**
- **Main Body:** 12 pages or longer
- **Paper Size:** 11" x 8.5" (US Letter)
- **Margins:** 1-inch margin on all four sides
- **Spacing:** Double spacing throughout
- **Font Size:** 12 or smaller
- **Font Type:** Standard academic fonts (Times New Roman, Arial, Calibri)

### What Counts Toward Word Count

**INCLUDED in Main Body Word Count:**
- All text in introduction, body paragraphs, and conclusion
- In-text citations
- Headings and subheadings
- Text within tables (if tables are in main body)

**NOT INCLUDED in Main Body Word Count:**
- Title page
- Cover sheets
- References/Bibliography
- Acknowledgements
- Tables (as separate elements)
- Figures (as separate elements)
- Appendices

### Formatting Specifications

- **Paper Size:** 11" x 8.5" inches
- **Margins:** 1 inch on all sides (top, bottom, left, right)
- **Line Spacing:** Double spacing
- **Font Size:** 12 point or smaller
- **Font Type:** Professional academic fonts (Times New Roman, Arial, Calibri, Cambria)
- **Page Numbers:** Required (check APA format for placement)
- **Alignment:** Left-aligned text (not justified)

## 2. Op-Ed Writing Requirements

### Hook Strategies for Opening an Op-Ed

**Types of Hooks:**

1. **Personal Story Hook:**
   - Start with a personal anecdote or experience
   - Creates emotional connection with readers
   - Example: "When I first encountered..."

2. **Statistical Hook:**
   - Begin with a surprising statistic or data point
   - Grabs attention with numbers
   - Example: "One in three Americans..."

3. **Question Hook:**
   - Pose a thought-provoking question
   - Engages readers immediately
   - Example: "What if we could prevent..."

4. **Controversial Statement Hook:**
   - Make a bold, debatable claim
   - Sparks interest and debate
   - Example: "The current system is fundamentally broken..."

5. **Scenario/Vignette Hook:**
   - Paint a vivid picture of a situation
   - Helps readers visualize the issue
   - Example: "Imagine a world where..."

6. **Quotation Hook:**
   - Start with a relevant quote
   - Adds authority or interest
   - Example: "As Albert Einstein once said..."

**Best Practices for Opening Hooks:**
- Keep it concise (1-3 sentences)
- Make it relevant to your main argument
- Ensure it leads naturally into your thesis
- Avoid clichés or overused phrases

### Hook Strategies for Closing an Op-Ed

**Types of Closing Hooks:**

1. **Call-to-Action (CTA):**
   - Urge readers to take specific action
   - Example: "It's time we demand change from our policymakers..."

2. **Circular Structure:**
   - Return to the opening hook/story
   - Creates a sense of completion
   - Example: "Just as I began with the story of..."

3. **Forward-Looking Statement:**
   - Paint a vision of the future
   - Inspires hope or concern
   - Example: "If we don't act now, we face..."

4. **Rhetorical Question:**
   - End with a question that lingers
   - Encourages continued reflection
   - Example: "The question remains: will we act in time?"

5. **Powerful Statement:**
   - End with a memorable, impactful sentence
   - Leaves a lasting impression
   - Example: "The time for change is now, and it starts with us."

6. **Solution Summary:**
   - Briefly summarize the proposed solution
   - Reinforces main argument
   - Example: "By implementing these changes, we can..."

**Best Practices for Closing Hooks:**
- Reinforce your main thesis
- Leave readers with something to think about
- Connect back to your opening if using circular structure
- End on a strong, memorable note

### Title Format Rules

**Title Requirements:**
- **Capitalization:** Title case (capitalize major words)
  - Capitalize: First word, last word, and all major words
  - Do NOT capitalize: Articles (a, an, the), conjunctions (and, but, or), prepositions (in, on, at, for, etc.) unless they are the first or last word
- **Length:** Typically 8-12 words (check specific requirements)
- **Style:** Should be engaging and reflect the content
- **Format:** No quotation marks unless quoting someone
- **Placement:** Centered on title page (for reports) or at top of document (for Op-Eds)

**Title Examples:**
- ✅ "Silent Wars Under the Roof: Why AI Must Step in to Protect Well-Being"
- ✅ "The Future of Healthcare: Predictive Analytics and Patient Outcomes"
- ❌ "the future of healthcare: predictive analytics and patient outcomes" (incorrect capitalization)
- ❌ "A Study About Machine Learning" (too vague)

### Op-Ed Structure Requirements

**Standard Op-Ed Structure:**

1. **Introduction (15-20% of word count)**
   - Hook to grab attention
   - Context/background information
   - Thesis statement (your main argument)

2. **Body Paragraphs (60-70% of word count)**
   - 3-5 paragraphs supporting your thesis
   - Each paragraph should have:
     - Topic sentence
     - Evidence/examples
     - Analysis/explanation
     - Transition to next paragraph
   - Address counterarguments if relevant

3. **Conclusion (10-15% of word count)**
   - Restate thesis (in different words)
   - Summarize main points
   - Closing hook (call-to-action, forward-looking statement, etc.)

### Op-Ed Tone and Voice Requirements

- **Voice:** First person ("I," "we") is acceptable and often preferred
- **Tone:** 
  - Engaging and accessible
  - Authoritative but not condescending
  - Passionate but logical
  - Professional yet conversational
- **Audience:** General educated public (not just academics)
- **Purpose:** Persuade, inform, and inspire action

## 3. Data Management Plan (DMP) Requirements

### Must-Have Sections/Components

**Required Elements in a Data Management Plan:**

1. **Data Collection Methods**
   - Description of how data will be collected
   - Sources of data
   - Collection procedures

2. **Data Types and Formats**
   - Types of data (quantitative, qualitative, mixed)
   - File formats (CSV, JSON, Excel, etc.)
   - Data structure and organization

3. **Data Storage and Backup**
   - Where data will be stored (local, cloud, servers)
   - Backup procedures
   - Security measures
   - Access controls

4. **Data Sharing and Access**
   - Who will have access to the data
   - Sharing protocols
   - Privacy considerations
   - Data sharing agreements

5. **Data Preservation**
   - Long-term storage plans
   - Retention policies
   - Archiving procedures
   - Repository information (if applicable)

6. **Data Documentation**
   - Metadata standards
   - Codebooks or data dictionaries
   - Variable descriptions
   - Documentation of data processing steps

7. **Ethical Considerations**
   - IRB approval (if applicable)
   - Privacy protection measures
   - Informed consent procedures
   - Data anonymization/de-identification

8. **Data Quality Assurance**
   - Quality control measures
   - Validation procedures
   - Error checking methods

9. **Timeline**
   - Data collection timeline
   - Processing timeline
   - Sharing/publication timeline

10. **Responsibilities**
    - Who is responsible for each aspect
    - Roles and responsibilities
    - Contact information

### Formatting and Structure Requirements

- **Length:** Typically 2-5 pages (check specific assignment)
- **Format:** Professional, organized document
- **Structure:** Use clear headings and subheadings
- **Clarity:** Write in clear, specific language
- **Completeness:** Address all required components

### Example DMP Structure

```
1. Introduction
   - Purpose of the data management plan
   - Overview of the project

2. Data Collection
   - Methods and sources
   - Timeline

3. Data Organization
   - File naming conventions
   - Folder structure
   - Data formats

4. Data Storage and Backup
   - Storage locations
   - Backup procedures
   - Security measures

5. Data Sharing and Access
   - Access controls
   - Sharing protocols

6. Data Preservation
   - Long-term storage
   - Archiving plans

7. Ethical Considerations
   - Privacy protection
   - IRB compliance

8. Timeline and Responsibilities
   - Key dates
   - Responsible parties
```

- **Minimum: 3,000 words** (main body only)
- **Automatic Zero Score:** Reports with less than 3,000 words in main body receive 0 points
- **Recommended:** Aim for 3,500-4,500 words to ensure you meet requirements comfortably

### Page Requirements

- **Main Body:** 12 pages or longer

- **Total Pages:** Will be longer when including title page, references, tables, figures

**Document Structure:**
1. Title Page (not counted in main body)
2. Abstract (if required)

4. References/Bibliography (not counted in main body)

- Acknowledgements

## 5. APA Style Requirements

### In-Text Citation Format

**Basic Format:**
- (Author, Year) for paraphrased information
- (Author, Year, p. #) for direct quotes

**Examples:**
- One author: (Smith, 2023)
- Two authors: (Smith & Jones, 2023)
- Three or more authors: (Smith et al., 2023)
- Direct quote: (Smith, 2023, p. 45)

**Narrative Citations:**
- Smith (2023) found that...
- According to Smith and Jones (2023)...

### Reference List Formatting

**Basic Format:**
- Alphabetical order by author's last name
- Hanging indent (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented)
- Double spacing throughout
- Author, A. A. (Year). Title. Source.

**Journal Article Example:**
```
Smith, J. A., & Jones, B. B. (2023). Machine learning applications in healthcare. 
Journal of Data Science, 15(3), 123-145. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx
```

**Book Example:**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023). Introduction to machine learning. Academic Press.
```

**Website Example:**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023, March 15). Understanding data management. 
Data Science Institute. https://www.example.com/article
```

### Title Page Format

**Required Elements:**
- Title of paper (centered, bold, title case)
- Author name
- Institutional affiliation (SJSU)
- Course number and name
- Instructor name
- Date

**Formatting:**
- Centered on page
- Double spaced
- Professional appearance
- No decorative elements

### Running Head Requirements

- **Format:** "RUNNING HEAD: Your Title" (left-aligned, all caps)
- **Page Numbers:** Right-aligned, same line as running head
- **First Page:** "Running head: YOUR TITLE" (with "Running head:" label)
- **Subsequent Pages:** "YOUR TITLE" (without "Running head:" label)

### Page Numbering Rules

- **Location:** Top right corner of every page
- **Format:** Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...)
- **First Page:** Usually page 1 (title page may be separate)
- **Consistency:** Number all pages consecutively

### Table and Figure Formatting

**Tables:**
- Numbered (Table 1, Table 2, etc.)
- Descriptive title above table
- Note below table if needed
- Referenced in text before appearing

**Figures:**
- Numbered (Figure 1, Figure 2, etc.)
- Descriptive caption below figure
- Referenced in text before appearing
- High quality and readable

## 7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Formatting Errors

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Incorrect margins:** Using margins other than 1 inch
2. **Wrong spacing:** Single spacing instead of double spacing
3. **Font size too large:** Using font larger than 12 point
4. **Incorrect paper size:** Using A4 or other sizes instead of 8.5" x 11"
5. **Missing page numbers:** Forgetting to number pages
6. **Inconsistent formatting:** Mixing different fonts or sizes

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Set up document formatting before writing
- Use document templates if provided
- Double-check formatting before submission
- Use "Format Painter" to ensure consistency

### Word Count Violations

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Including excluded elements:** Counting references, title page, etc. toward word count
2. **Not meeting minimum:** Submitting less than 3,000 words in main body
3. **Incorrect word count:** Using character count instead of word count
4. **Not verifying:** Not checking actual word count before submission

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Use word count tool in your word processor
- Count only main body text (introduction, body, conclusion)
- Aim for 3,500+ words to have a buffer
- Verify word count multiple times during writing process

### Citation Errors

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Missing citations:** Not citing sources for paraphrased information
2. **Incorrect format:** Not following APA style correctly
3. **Incomplete references:** Missing information in reference list
4. **Mismatched citations:** In-text citation doesn't match reference list
5. **Using Wikipedia:** Citing Wikipedia (explicitly not allowed)

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Cite every source you use
- Use citation management tools (Zotero, Mendeley, etc.)
- Double-check APA format for each citation type
- Verify all in-text citations have corresponding references
- Use only acceptable sources

### Structure Problems

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Weak opening:** Not using an effective hook
2. **No clear thesis:** Missing or unclear thesis statement
3. **Poor organization:** Disorganized paragraphs or sections
4. **Weak conclusion:** Not reinforcing main argument
5. **Missing transitions:** Abrupt changes between paragraphs

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Plan structure before writing
- Use outline to organize thoughts
- Ensure each paragraph has clear purpose
- Use transition sentences between paragraphs
- Revise for flow and coherence

### Op-Ed Specific Mistakes

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Too academic:** Using overly formal, academic language
2. **No hook:** Starting without attention-grabbing opening
3. **Weak closing:** Ending without call-to-action or memorable statement
4. **Poor title:** Vague or unengaging title
5. **Too long/short:** Not meeting word count requirements

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Write for general audience, not just academics
- Start with strong hook
- End with impactful closing
- Create engaging, specific title
- Check word count requirements

### DMP Specific Mistakes

**❌ Common Mistakes:**
1. **Missing sections:** Not including all required components
2. **Vague descriptions:** Not being specific enough
3. **No timeline:** Missing timeline information
4. **Unclear responsibilities:** Not specifying who does what
5. **Missing ethical considerations:** Not addressing privacy/IRB issues

**✅ How to Avoid:**
- Use checklist of required sections
- Be specific and detailed
- Include clear timeline
- Assign clear responsibilities
- Address all ethical considerations

## 8. Grading Criteria

### Point Allocation

**Individual Project Report (30% of course grade):**
- Project Draft Reports: 5%
- Other GWAR tasks: 5%
- Final Project Reports: 20%

**Breakdown of Final Project Report:**
- Content and analysis: ~40-50%
- Writing quality: ~20-30%
- Formatting and structure: ~15-20%
- Citations and references: ~10-15%

### Automatic Zero Scores

**Conditions for Zero Score:**
1. **Word count violation:** Main body less than 3,000 words
2. **Plagiarism:** Any form of academic dishonesty
3. **Missing submission:** Not submitted by deadline
4. **Format violation:** Severe formatting errors (check specific requirements)
5. **Unacceptable sources:** Using Wikipedia or other prohibited sources extensively

### Rubric Expectations

**Excellent (A range):**
- Meets or exceeds all word/page requirements
- Perfect or near-perfect formatting
- Strong, clear thesis and argument
- Excellent writing quality
- Proper citations throughout
- Engaging and well-structured
- No major errors

**Good (B range):**
- Meets all requirements
- Minor formatting issues
- Clear thesis and argument
- Good writing quality
- Mostly proper citations
- Well-structured
- Few minor errors

**Satisfactory (C range):**
- Meets minimum requirements
- Some formatting issues
- Basic thesis and argument
- Adequate writing quality
- Some citation errors
- Acceptable structure
- Several errors present

**Needs Improvement (D/F range):**
- Does not meet all requirements
- Significant formatting issues
- Weak or unclear thesis
- Poor writing quality
- Many citation errors
- Poor structure
- Many errors throughout

### Key Evaluation Criteria

**Content:**
- Depth of analysis
- Quality of research
- Originality of insights
- Relevance to topic
- Use of evidence

**Writing:**
- Clarity and coherence
- Grammar and mechanics
- Style and tone
- Organization
- Flow and transitions

**Formatting:**
- Adherence to requirements
- Consistency
- Professional appearance
- Proper use of APA style

**Citations:**
- Accuracy
- Completeness
- Proper formatting
- Appropriate sources
